Linears vs Tactiles vs Clickies

Chances are, if you're interested in mechanical keyboards, you know the basic three switches: Cherry MX Reds, Cherry MX Browns, and Cherry MX blues. These switches are extremely popular and well known by even the most casual of mechanical keyboard owners. But what is the difference between these switches under the hood?

Linear switches (Cherry MX Reds) are switches that do not have a click action, or a tactile bump. These switches are smooth on the downward stroke, and are often used for gaming applications, to achieve fast presses.

Tactile switches (Cherry MX Browns) are switches that do not have a click action, but do have a tactile bump. These switches are initially heavy to press, but after passing the tactile bump, become smooth, akin to a linear switch. The bump at the actuation point allows you to 'feel' when the switch is pressed, giving you a more feedback, and is great feature for an avid typist.

Clicky switches (Cherry MX Blues) are switches with a tactile bump, as well as a click action. These switches are similar to a tactile switch, but also have a click action at the tactile bump, giving the user sound feedback to indicate the keypress was successfully registered.

Cherry MX Red

Cherry MX Reds are the king of gaming switches. Popularized by keyboards such as the Corsair K65, this is a linear switch with a low actuation force and no bump, allowing for fast, precise clicks when playing games. The lack of a bump also greatly assists the switch in gaining users outside of gaming- the silent, no click design, being highly sought after for normal work.

Technical Data

  • 45 cN operating force
  • 2.0 mm pre travel
  • 4.0 mm total travel
  • No click

Cherry MX Brown

Being another option for the Corsair K65, Cherry Browns found themselves rocketing to popularity. Achieving a subtle, yet satisfying tactile feel, the switch is very popular as an alternative for gamers who also desire feedback when typing. The switch is only slightly heavier than Cherry's linear offering, but provides a more rounded experience.

Technical Data

  • 55 cN operating force
  • 2.0 mm pre travel
  • 4.0 mm total travel
  • No click

Cherry MX Blue

Cherry Blues are extremely divisive among the mechanical keyboard community. Providing substantial audible feedback on each click becomes quite annoying to your fellow coworkers and classmates. Despite this, the switch is extremely popular, becoming available during the advent of the consumer mechanical keyboard market, the switch still sees wide use today.

Technical Data

  • 60 cN operating force
  • 2.2 mm pre travel
  • 4.0 mm total travel
  • With click

Always be on the lookout for what switch will specifically suit your own taste.
